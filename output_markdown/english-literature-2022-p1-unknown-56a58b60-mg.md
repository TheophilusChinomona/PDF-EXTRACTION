## SECTION 1

### Question 1(a) [15 marks] (Passage-based Analysis)

**Coriolanus - Act 1 Scene 1 Passage Analysis**

> **Marker instruction:** AO2 is dominant (75%). AO1 is 25%. Use Level Descriptors Section 1, part (a): Shakespeare.

- Striking nature of this opening scene (linguistic and visual impact).
- Economy of achievement: a great deal conveyed in few opening lines.
- Mood of turmoil and division across the social divide and within the plebeian class.
- Theme and tone of disharmony emphasised in the disjointed prose spoken by the Citizens.
- Sophistication in character: 1 Citizen's use of rhetorical patterning.
- Decided views of Coriolanus and Volumnia versus ambiguous attitudes elsewhere.
- Menenius’s characteristic appearance trying to soothe citizens' unrest.
- Staging possibilities involving a large cast and generating potent visual impact.

*Note: Indicative content is not prescriptive nor exclusive.*

### Question 1(b) [15 marks] (Whole Play Analysis)

**Coriolanus - Social Structures**

> **Marker instruction:** AO1 and AO5 are equally weighted (50% each). Use Level Descriptors Section 1, part (b): Shakespeare.

- Menenius’s image of the 'body politic' (the 'parable of the belly').
- Social division in Rome between the patricians and the plebeians.
- Wide range of social structures informing the dramatic development of events.
- Consideration of different interpretations, such as the 2011 film as critical commentary.
- Marxist or other political interpretations regarding social hierarchy.
- Focus on how structures control events rather than purely historical context.

### Question 2(a) [15 marks] (Passage-based Analysis)

**Hamlet - Act 1 Scene 3 Passage Analysis**

> **Marker instruction:** AO2 is dominant (75%). AO1 is 25%.

- Context of Laertes’ imminent departure for France.
- Troubled relationship between Ophelia and Hamlet dominating the passage.
- Domestic nature of the scene as part of dramatic effects.
- Polonius as a character who divides opinion; linguistic usage vs character interpretation.
- Polonius’ celebrated advice: listing techniques, sententious qualities, irony, and humour.
- Laertes’ intriguingly monosyllabic response to his father.
- Contrast between gentle tone of brother/sister and vigorous dialogue with Polonius.
- Polonius foregrounding worldly wisdom against Ophelia’s 'green girl' naivety.

### Question 2(b) [15 marks] (Whole Play Analysis)

**Hamlet - Parental Control**

> **Marker instruction:** AO1 and AO5 are equally weighted (50% each).

- Polonius’ portrayal as tedious, domineering, sententious, or silly in controlling his children.
- The 'first family': Hamlet’s relationship with Gertrude, King Hamlet (Ghost), and Claudius.
- Claudius as a stepfather keen to get Hamlet out of the way.
- Ghost of King Hamlet demanding revenge as a form of control.
- Fortinbras/Old Fortinbras element as a secondary parallel (not expected but valid).
- Hamlet’s attempts to exert control over his mother (e.g., Closet Scene).
- Performance interpretations of parental dynamics.

### Question 3(a) [15 marks] (Passage-based Analysis)

**Measure for Measure - Act 3 Scene 2 Passage Analysis**

> **Marker instruction:** AO2 is dominant (75%). AO1 is 25%.

- Intimacy provided by the absence of other characters in the dialogue.
- Use of dramatic irony regarding Lucio’s ignorance of the Friar’s true identity.
- Generation of comedy and links to the final scene's resolution.
- Variety of tone: Lucio’s ripe/earthy prose, puns, and bawdy imagery.
- Lucio's insults addressed inappropriately to a priest.
- Duke Vincentio remaining mysterious, brusque, and firmly within disguise.
- Duke's final speech as an unsettling premonition of the justice he will mete out.

### Question 3(b) [15 marks] (Whole Play Analysis)

**Measure for Measure - Kinds of Dishonesty**

> **Marker instruction:** AO1 and AO5 are equally weighted (50% each).

- Duke Vincentio’s disguise and contrivances (e.g., the bed trick).
- Moral effects of deceptions: deceptions seen as reprehensible or cowardly in some productions.
- The professional dissembling of 'low-life' characters.
- Angelo’s hypocrisy as a straightforward kind of dishonesty.
- Lucio’s inherent dishonesty as a character trait.
- Ambiguity between 'dishonesty' and 'white lies' within the play's morality.

### Question 4(a) [15 marks] (Passage-based Analysis)

**Richard III - Act 3 Scene 7 Passage Analysis**

> **Marker instruction:** AO2 is dominant (75%). AO1 is 25%.

- Gloucester being offered the crown as a key moment in the dramatic arc.
- Rhetorical figures and linguistic ironies in Buckingham’s major speech.
- Richard’s disguise of pious reticence.
- The role of the Mayor and the staging for an onstage audience.
- Buckingham’s designs on 'Edward’s bastard children' (Princes in the Tower).
- Visual image: Richard elevated on stage between two bishops.
- Metatheatrical elements: the scene as an act of managed subterfuge.

### Question 4(b) [15 marks] (Whole Play Analysis)

**Richard III - Devaluation of Kingship**

> **Marker instruction:** AO1 and AO5 are equally weighted (50% each).

- Evaluation of Richard’s qualities, faults, cynicism, and dishonesty.
- The 'golden yoke of sovereignty' as Richard's primary goal.
- Intimacy with the audience devaluing the 'magic' of monarchy.
- Buckingham’s complicity in devaluing the office.
- The previous King (Edward IV) as a worn-out libertine.
- Historical context of the Wars of the Roses (deposing/restoring kings).
- Richmond's victory as a restoration of value (Tudor dynasty).
- Interpretations: Olivier (1955), Ian McKellen (1995), Teenage Dick (Donmar Warehouse).

### Question 5(a) [15 marks] (Passage-based Analysis)

**The Tempest - Act 1 Scene 2 Passage Analysis**

> **Marker instruction:** AO2 is dominant (75%). AO1 is 25%.

- Intimacy of the introductory scene between Miranda and Prospero.
- Prospero clarifying narrative back-story for the audience's benefit.
- Establishment of Antonio’s guilt, framing the play as a revenge drama.
- Contrast between Act 1 Scene 1 (bustle) and Act 1 Scene 2 (calm rationality).
- Prospero’s role as teacher and controlling magician.
- Miranda’s subservience and passivity (completing Prospero’s verse lines).
- Catechistic tone and atmosphere in the exchange.

### Question 5(b) [15 marks] (Whole Play Analysis)

**The Tempest - Cruelty and Ruthlessness**

> **Marker instruction:** AO1 and AO5 are equally weighted (50% each).

- Responsibility for cruelty: Caliban (nature/nurture debate).
- Hardened courtly criminals: Antonio and Sebastian.
- Distinction between 'cruelty' (pleasure in pain) and 'ruthlessness' (indifference to suffering).
- Play as a setting where human nature is put under a microscope.
- Postcolonial theories regarding the treatment of indigenous characters.
- Personalized responses justified by textual examples.

### Question 6(a) [15 marks] (Passage-based Analysis)

**Twelfth Night - Act 3 Scene 4 Passage Analysis**

> **Marker instruction:** AO2 is dominant (75%). AO1 is 25%.

- Interruption of the duel between Viola (Cesario) and Sir Andrew by Antonio’s arrest.
- Complexity of blocking involving a large number of characters.
- Language of mistaken identity and confusion dominating the passage.
- Antonio’s passionate and devoted character versus Viola’s valuation of 'ingratitude'.
- Blunt professionalism of the Officers' interjections.
- Atmosphere of urgency: short lines, exclamations, and misunderstandings.
- Gender confusion: Viola in male disguise.

### Question 6(b) [15 marks] (Whole Play Analysis)

**Twelfth Night - Humour and Suffering**

> **Marker instruction:** AO1 and AO5 are equally weighted (50% each).

- Mistaken identity as a contributor to plot and thematic development.
- Modern interpretations of Antonio’s devotion to Sebastian (e.g., closeted homosexuality).
- Malvolio’s disguise: amusing to audience but painful to character.
- The 'mixed quality' of Shakespearean comedy (humour vs suffering).
- Identity conflicts involving gender.
- Performance choices: single-sex, gender-reversed, or 'gender-blind' castings.

## SECTION 2

### Question 7

**Topic:** Intellect and Judgement (Drama and Poetry pre-1900)

**Introduction:**
- Literature shows that clever people do not always make the wisest choices.
- Exploration of the relationship between characters' intellect and judgement.

**Body:**

*Drama Texts*
  - Edward II: Intelligence does not help the King with public role decisions.
  - The Duchess of Malfi: Bosola’s brains are employed in dubious exploits.
  - She Stoops to Conquer: Clever interlopers end in scrapes; the wise character is the fool Tony Lumpkin.
  - A Doll’s House: Nora makes mistakes initially but grows wiser than her husband.
  - An Ideal Husband: Intelligent people get into unwise scrapes on their route to success.

*Poetry Texts*
  - The Merchant’s Prologue and Tale: No link between age and intelligence; cynical view of wisdom.
  - Paradise Lost: Satan’s high intelligence leads to damnation; Adam and Eve's negative decision.
  - Coleridge: Wise contemplation preferred over philosophic enquiry; Ancient Mariner repents a single decision.
  - Tennyson’s Maud: Intelligent speculations end in deep unhappiness.
  - Rossetti: Wisdom of God vs. foolishness of human intellectual conceit.

**Conclusion:**
- Relationship between intellect and judgment varies across genres, often showing a disconnect.

### Question 8

**Topic:** Social Structures vs. Natural World

**Introduction:**
- Literature has more to say about social structures than about the natural world.

**Body:**

*Drama Texts*
  - Edward II / Duchess of Malfi: Human intrigue and social structures as core interest.
  - She Stoops to Conquer: Fashionable society confronts the Pastoral world.
  - A Doll’s House: Set in rural Norway but focused on social action.
  - Wilde: An artificial world where nature is largely irrelevant (buttonholes and house-plants).

*Poetry Texts*
  - Merchant’s Tale: Januarie’s symbolic, self-contained garden as backdrop for intrigue.
  - Paradise Lost: The Garden of Eden as a setting for social interaction.
  - Coleridge: Nature dominates 'conversation' and teaches lessons.
  - Maud: Natural settings as a projection of the narrator's mind.
  - Rossetti: Natural settings representing the limitations of a 'fallen' world.

**Conclusion:**
- Balance between societal commentary and environmental presence.

### Question 9

**Topic:** Powerful Emotions and Unacceptable Behaviour

**Introduction:**
- Powerful emotions are often the excuse for unacceptable behaviour.

**Body:**

*Drama Texts*
  - Edward II: The King is ruled by emotions leading to unacceptable results.
  - Duchess of Malfi: Repressed emotions drive characters to unacceptable behaviour.
  - She Stoops to Conquer: Emotions goad characters into crossing social boundaries.
  - A Doll’s House: Nora's emotions as the driving force deemed 'unacceptable' by husband.

*Poetry Texts*
  - Merchant’s Tale: Januarie fits the template of emotions driving deeds.
  - Paradise Lost: Independence, love, and pride leading to the Fall.
  - Coleridge: Power of emotions as a celebrated Romantic ideal vs condemnation.
  - Maud: Dangers of unchecked emotions vs the insights they bring.
  - Rossetti: Verse often condemns strong emotions as missing their object.

**Conclusion:**
- Emotions serve as both a catalyst for action and a justification for transgressions.

### Question 10

**Topic:** Discipline and Self-Discipline

**Introduction:**
- Discipline is most effective when it comes from within.

**Body:**

*Drama Texts*
  - Edward II: Focus on society-imposed discipline on transgressive individuals.
  - Duchess of Malfi: The Duchess learns self-discipline through suffering; Bosola lacks it.
  - She Stoops to Conquer: Kate’s self-discipline sustains her in a chaotic world.
  - A Doll’s House: Nora rejects societal constraints for internal vision of truth.

*Poetry Texts*
  - Merchant’s Tale: Characters fail to demonstrate self-discipline.
  - Paradise Lost: Lack of self-control in parents leads to strict external discipline (death).
  - Coleridge: Self-discipline acquired through the Ancient Mariner's painful experiences.
  - Rossetti’s Goblin Market: Self-discipline as the means to overcome temptation.

**Conclusion:**
- Internal vs external discipline as a recurring moral conflict.

### Question 11

**Topic:** Generosity

**Introduction:**
- Characters in literature often behave in unexpectedly generous ways.

**Body:**

*Drama Texts*
  - Edward II: Acts of love as a form of generosity (Queen Isabella).
  - Duchess of Malfi: Sensitivity and empathy in the Duchess’s own behaviour.
  - She Stoops to Conquer: Guileless generosity of the provinces vs. sophisticated selfishness.
  - An Ideal Husband: Generous acts from Lord Goring save individuals from disaster.

*Poetry Texts*
  - Merchant’s Tale: Notable for its lack of generosity and celebration of cynicism.
  - Paradise Lost: Generosity of spirit in the relationship between Eve and Adam.
  - Coleridge: Instances of material, moral, and spiritual generosity (turning point of Ancient Mariner).
  - Rossetti: Celebration of generosity on personal, communal, and religious levels.

**Conclusion:**
- Generosity as a redemptive or surprising character trait.

### Question 12

**Topic:** Religion and Human Motivation

**Introduction:**
- Religion as a key element in human motivation and depictive religious belief.

**Body:**

*Drama Texts*
  - Edward II: Religious leaders less important than political barons.
  - Duchess of Malfi: Persecution by a corrupt Cardinal; Protestant play condemning Catholic Italy.
  - A Doll’s House: Lutheran Church provides the moral backdrop that stifles Nora.
  - An Ideal Husband: Social usage and aestheticism over Christian attitudes.

*Poetry Texts*
  - Merchant’s Tale: Focus on classical figures (Venus, Pluto) rather than conventional religion.
  - Paradise Lost: Epic re-working of Judeo-Christian faith and the origins of prayer.
  - Coleridge: Mystical views and parables of sin and expiation.
  - Rossetti: Religion plays the key role in motivation and poetic world.

**Conclusion:**
- Religion as a societal backdrop and a driver of internal character ethics.
