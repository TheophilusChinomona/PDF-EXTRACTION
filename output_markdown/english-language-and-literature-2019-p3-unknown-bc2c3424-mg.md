## Section A

### Question 1 [32 marks] (Essay)

**In what ways does the writer of your text use time?**

> **Marker instruction:** A response that does not address any one of the three assessment objectives (AO1, AO2, AO3) targeted cannot achieve all of the marks in the given level. Use 'best fit' to locate the level descriptor.

**Introduction:**
- Answers should range across the text to explore how time is manipulated.
- Identify the role time plays in the novel as a whole and the broader generic context.

**Body:**

*Jane Eyre (AO2 and AO1)*
  - Time passes from Jane aged 10 to Jane established in her marriage to Rochester.
  - Autobiographical voice (Bildungsroman) comments on what the child saw compared with what the woman later judged.
  - Rites of passage novel structure moving from early years to time present.
  - Periods of Jane’s life are individuated sharply to illustrate stages on her spiritual journey.
  - Sober narrator uses distance of time to contrast the 'sanguine, brilliant, careless, romping child' with the adult self.

*Jane Eyre (AO3)*
  - Historical novel covering fifteen years of the pre-Victorian period.
  - Abuses (e.g. Mr Brocklehurst) are fixed in the near past and viewed as trauma.
  - Gothic elements reflect Romanticism and the Gothic novel tradition.
  - Transcendence of contemporary stereotypes through romance.

*The Great Gatsby (AO2 and AO1)*
  - Narrator Nick tells his story consecutively but with numerous loops in time for backstory.
  - Gatsby lives partly in the past and is reconstructing it in time present.
  - Flashes of a simpler Gatsby at earlier periods (e.g. with Dan Cody or diary entry).
  - Time is telescoped in final pages reaching from mid-western settlers to first Dutchmen.
  - Prose conjures earlier periods; the long summer serves as a temporal backdrop.

*The Great Gatsby (AO3)*
  - Set during Prohibition, after WWI, with an atmosphere of impending catastrophe.
  - Characters like Tom and Daisy are caught in the present; Gatsby looks back to innocence.
  - Suggestion that time can be cheated temporarily by 'successful gestures'.

*Things Fall Apart (AO2 and AO1)*
  - Timeframe shielded in the first section; time counted in seasons and rituals.
  - Life seen as a series of transition rites bringing one nearer to ancestors.
  - Simple prose communicates tragedy and joy; proverbs and fables are integrated.
  - History enters in second and third sections with missionaries and colonisers.
  - Novel as a fable about the impossibility of dragging traditional culture into the 20th century in one generation.

*Things Fall Apart (AO3)*
  - Reflects history of missionaries in Nigeria (1860s) and colonial rule (1880s).
  - Okonkwo's conversion to Christianity contrasted with indigenous creeds of honour-killing.
  - District Commissioner's 'pacifying' processes at the end.

*The God of Small Things (AO1 and AO2)*
  - Rejection of colonial memories for post-colonial India's contemporary problems.
  - Non-linear handling of time compresses and transcends history.
  - Cultural setting dwarfs characters; Rahel's development as focaliser holds events at micro-level.
  - Disjointed events in coherent order mirror complex cultural problems.

*The God of Small Things (AO3)*
  - Timeframe moves between 1969 (separation) and 1993 (reunion).
  - Irregular unfolding of memories concentrates story on the twins.
  - Sense of time passing brought to a standstill.

*Atonement (AO1 and AO2)*
  - Discrete sections: historical sequences (1935, 1940), wish-fulfilment (WWII), and coda (1999).
  - Briony's control highlights the responsibilities of the fiction writer.
  - Wartime sections use terse prose ('bleakest realism').
  - Opening pages use flourishing symbolism derived from a 'teenage novella'.

*Atonement (AO3)*
  - Class tensions (Robbie Turner vs upper-class) reminiscent of Victorian/Edwardian novels.
  - Graphic handling of retreat from Dunkirk and wartime nursing.
  - Embodiment of desperate moral choices of wartime.

*The Namesake (AO1 and AO2)*
  - Journey narrative with biographical format; hero as focaliser.
  - Free indirect discourse splices in parents' opinions.
  - Gogol's name anxieties form rites of passage and structure the book.
  - Precise dates matter in family history.
  - Exploration of immigrant lives built on changing attitudes over years.

*The Namesake (AO3)*
  - Background of American history (Vietnam to Millennium) and recreational drugs.
  - Concentration on family ties over professional interests.
  - Distance between Indian culture and the American 'world'.

**Conclusion:**
- Summarize how the manipulation of time serves the novel’s themes and structural integrity.

*Note: Assessment Objectives: AO2 (6%), AO1 (5%), AO3 (5%). Total 16%. Dominant AO is AO2.*

### Question 2 [32 marks] (Essay)

**How does the writer of your text use contrasts?**

> **Marker instruction:** A response that does not address any one of the three assessment objectives (AO1, AO2, AO3) targeted cannot achieve all of the marks in the given level.

**Introduction:**
- Explore how contrasts are created and the role they play in the novel as a whole.
- Relate contrasts to the broader generic context.

**Body:**

*Jane Eyre (AO2 and AO1)*
  - Uncompromising judgements of a child’s world at Lowood vs Brocklehurst’s lifestyle.
  - Spirituality of Helen Burns contrasted with St John Rivers.
  - Contrasts between Gothic imaginings and terrible truths.
  - Marriage offers from Rivers and Rochester as contrasting romance formats.
  - Stylistic use of oxymorons (e.g. 'pure gold, with a steely point of agony').

*Jane Eyre (AO3)*
  - Male freedom vs Jane’s Puritanical self-restraint.
  - Gentility of the governess vs grand airs of the Ingrams.
  - Jane’s integrity vs arbitrary authority figures.
  - Worldly vs spiritual callings in liberal Victorian evangelicalism.

*The Great Gatsby (AO2 and AO1)*
  - Eastern backgrounds vs New York metropolitan playground.
  - Valley of Ashes (poverty/decrepitude) vs glamour of Gatsby’s parties.
  - Gatsby as a walking contradiction (Platonic essence vs bootlegger).
  - Large parties as 'loneliest places'.
  - Daisy's gleam vs struggles of the poor (social satire).

*The Great Gatsby (AO3)*
  - Roaring twenties cynical song-lyrics vs grinding poverty of immigrants.
  - Moral reform/Prohibition vs fortunes made by breaking the law.

*Things Fall Apart (AO2 and AO1)*
  - Linguistic contrasts between Okonkwo’s feelings and simple narrative language.
  - Gender difference: male brutality vs serene domestic village life.
  - Oral culture (folk-tales) vs imposed written English (bureaucrats/missionaries).
  - Tribal needs vs colonial designs.

*Things Fall Apart (AO3)*
  - Decision to write in English vs Nigerian dialect (linguistic paradox).
  - English as a tool that commemorates the process of colonisation.

*The God of Small Things (AO1 and AO2)*
  - Free prose/time tricks vs robust traditional 'love laws'.
  - Malayalam dialect vs English (lingua franca).
  - Cultural conflicts: Catholic asceticism, communism, and the caste system.
  - Acceptance of 'Untouchable' status vs individual desire.

*The God of Small Things (AO3)*
  - Linguistic registers (child talk vs old English hegemony).
  - Lush descriptions of scent/sound/colour vs small tragedies (epic vs microcosmic).

*Atonement (AO1 and AO2)*
  - Pallid prose of Section 1 vs fierce reportage of later sections.
  - Tensions between the real world of action and metafictional aesthetic motives.
  - Cyril Connolly's 'crystalline present moment' vs Robbie’s fatal progress in Dunkirk.
  - Briony's life as a writer vs the 'huge fact of death'.

*Atonement (AO3)*
  - Briony’s successful fiction vs her tangle of shame and frustration.
  - Privileged upbringing vs a war where 'everyone was guilty and no-one was'.

*The Namesake (AO1 and AO2)*
  - Rewards and difficulties of multiculturalism.
  - Unambitious, 'arms-length' prose vs complex identity shifts.
  - Ancestral culture vs American pop culture and sexual revolution.
  - Setting contrasts: sub-continental scale (India) vs architectural décor of living spaces.

*The Namesake (AO3)*
  - Liberalisation in up-market American life vs Bengali family culture.
  - Gogol’s name change compared to 'revolutionaries and transvestites'.

**Conclusion:**
- Evaluate how contrasts sharpen the novel's themes or characterizations.
