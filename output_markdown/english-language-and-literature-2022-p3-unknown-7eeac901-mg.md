## SECTION A

### Question 1 [32 marks] (Essay)

**In what ways does the opening of the novel set the narrative in motion?**

> **Marker instruction:** A higher level response (levels 4 to 6) will: Explore the ways the writer establishes key features of the text; Use vocabulary, terminology and narrative concepts appropriately; Show an understanding of the literary and generic context. A lower level response (level 1 – 3) will: Identify and list some ways; Use some appropriate vocabulary; Show limited understanding of the literary and generic context.

**Introduction:**
- Discussion of a novel’s ‘opening’ is not restricted to the first page or first chapter, but to all early developments in the book and the way these relate to what follows.

**Body:**

*Jane Eyre*
  - The novel opens by plunging into an account of life at Gateshead Hall.
  - Jane establishes herself as controlling the narrative of her life.
  - Establishes credentials as fictional autobiography and bildungsroman.
  - Red Room episode anticipates further Gothic writing later in the story.
  - Jane’s tone suggests she is now a balanced, happily married lady looking back.
  - Jane’s insights into the dysfunctional family of the Reeds.
  - Confronts and morally overcomes Mrs Reed at age ten showing rectitude and feistiness.
  - Lowood sequence sharpened with personal reminiscence of the Brontë sisters’ time at Cowan Bridge.
  - Contrast between Jane’s independent spirit and the non-judgmental pacifism of Helen Burns.

*The Great Gatsby*
  - Masterpiece of compression; Nick Carraway established as the filter/narrator at once.
  - Nick is fresh from World War One and looking to be ‘something in the City’.
  - Restrained style of opening chapters except for the description of Dr T.J. Eckleburg.
  - Gatsby as an ‘absent presence’, previewed by Nick as always in the forefront of his mind.
  - Gatsby is likely to be more socially mobile and impactful than other characters.
  - Lush inventive style exemplified by ambitious metaphors (e.g., New York trees).

*Things Fall Apart*
  - Teasingly simple style requires ‘delayed decoding’ from the reader.
  - Pre-colonial world of the Igbo tribe restricted to ‘nine villages’.
  - Okonkwo’s prowess stems from martial deeds, making him a celebrity at eighteen.
  - Prose dominated by simple declarative writing, avoiding the abstract register.
  - Okonkwo established as insecure in his ‘achievement’, which can make him brutal.
  - Ikemefuma introduced early as an object of that brutality.

*The God of Small Things*
  - Opening section ‘Paradise, Pickles and Preserves’ shows enchanted existence of twins Estha and Rahel.
  - Lush, metaphorical prose reminiscent of Romantic accounts of childhood (e.g., Laurie Lee).
  - Drowning of Sophie Mol introduced early with the shadow of modern Indian culture.
  - Narrative serendipity and post-modern writing; the story has ‘no secrets’.
  - Unclear ‘contract’ with the reader at the start.

*Atonement*
  - Establishment of apprentice novelist Briony Tallis’s adolescent inner life.
  - Briony’s struggle to get Lola and the twins to participate in her play.
  - Language simulates the awkward pretension of very young writers.
  - Exposition misconstrues a ‘sexual misadventure’ in a Country House novel atmosphere.
  - Prose weaving a sunlit ‘geometry of light’ reminiscent of Forster/Woolf.
  - Broken Meissen vase recalls Henry James’ The Golden Bowl.
  - Focus on a novelist writing a novel points up the distance between art and life.

*The Namesake*
  - Begins with the birth of Gogol in a high-tech hospital in Cambridge, Massachusetts.
  - Mother Ashima forced to swap ‘Murshidabad sari’ for hospital gown.
  - Ashoke mulls over Russian literature and Gogol’s ‘The Overcoat’.
  - Sequence provides early explanation of the novel’s title.
  - Bengali customs lie cheek-by-jowl with New England culture (e.g., Mother Goose haircut).
  - Slickly accessible style reminiscent of short stories in The New Yorker.

### Question 2 [32 marks] (Essay)

**In what ways does the writer of your text use symbols and motifs?**

> **Marker instruction:** A higher level response will: Explore the ways the writer uses symbols and motifs beyond obvious features; Use narrative concepts appropriately; Show an understanding of literary and generic context.

**Introduction:**
- Candidates should range across the text to explore how symbols and motifs are used and the function they play in the novel as a whole.

**Body:**

*Jane Eyre*
  - Motif of the ‘fire of passion’ (Jane’s feelings, lightning on oak tree, Bertha as Firestarter, purgatorial flame).
  - Metaphors of imprisonment and repression (Bertha’s attic).
  - Spartan motifs at Lowood (forced haircuts, black pillar Brocklehurst).
  - Gothic implications of the Red Room and shadows at Thornfield.
  - Jane’s Romantic imagery in her pictures (Arctic wastes).

*The Great Gatsby*
  - Green light at the end of Daisy’s dock as a symbol of hopes/American Dream.
  - Minatory symbols: eyes of Dr T.J. Eckleburg and the Valley of Ashes.
  - Symbols of fatal leisure (swimming-pool).
  - Overheated/overwrought symbolism generated by summer weather and setting.
  - Languid ominousness.

*Things Fall Apart*
  - Avoids imagery where possible to distance from European novels about Africa.
  - Focuses on symbols physically embedded in text like ‘Roaring Flame’ (Okonkwo).
  - Beast fables (Tortoise and Cat) assume symbolic value.
  - Cultural symbols: masks covering elders playing resurrected ancestors.
  - Locusts as an uncomfortable prelude to the killing of Ikemefuna.

*The God of Small Things*
  - Power of symbols on childhood experience.
  - The History House as a cultural crossroads and scene of dark adventures.
  - Grandiose landscape descriptions: toxic river and skull-like bed.
  - Pappachi’s moth as a symbol of unrecognized achievement and injustice.
  - Rahul’s watch, frozen at ten to two, symbolizes time vs. timelessness.

*Atonement*
  - Uncle Clem’s Vase as a symbol of the past/heirloom, echoing Henry James.
  - Fountain and Temple as a theatrical, symbolic setting for Briony’s ‘crime’.
  - Amo Bars (Paul Marshall) as a motif of convenience and sexual offense.
  - Severed leg in the tree as a shock-value symbol in Dunkirk.
  - Dunkirk as a symbol of miraculous escape vs. historical facts beyond control.

*The Namesake*
  - Gogol’s name as a series of rites of passage and tight chronological structure.
  - Nicolai Gogol’s ‘The Overcoat’ as a cultural symbol of transcendence.
  - Journeys (road, rail, plane) vs. domestic points (the kitchen).
  - Bengali cuisine as a symbol of intercultural malaise.
  - Crumpled paper clutched in train wreck symbolizing Gogol’s personality.
